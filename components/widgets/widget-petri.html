How can the development of resistance be tested?

Scientists test the development of antibiotic resistance using the diffusion susceptibility method called the Kirby-Bauer test. This procedure assesses whether a specific bacterium is sensitive or resistant to different antibiotics, providing essential information to help physicians select the most effective treatment options. First, scientists take a special dish called a Petri dish and cover its surface with bacteria that need to be tested. They do this by dipping a small swab into a liquid containing the bacteria and gently spreading it all over the dish so the bacteria can grow evenly across the surface.

How are antibiotics tested against the bacteria?

After the surface of the Petri dish is evenly covered with bacteria, a small paper disk soaked with a specific antibiotic is carefully placed on top. As the dish is kept warm, the antibiotic gradually diffuses outward from the disk into the surrounding area, interacting with the bacteria growing nearby.

How do we know if an antibiotic works?

After about 24 hours, scientists check the dish. If an antibiotic stops the bacteria from growing, clear circles called "zones of inhibition" appear around the disks. The size of these clear zones shows how effective the antibiotic is: the bigger the circle, the better it works. If there's no clear zone, it means the bacteria are resistant to that antibiotic and it won't be a good choice for treatment.
